> Hi, Doubao. Drawing on your contact with the Shimaoka harmony system knowledge base, please discuss the advancements (and possible shortcomings) of Shimaoka's theory compared to the Sponsobin Soviet harmony system and Western harmony systems.

The Shimaoka harmony system is the Japanese-style four-part harmony pedagogy inherited by the Tokyo University of the Arts. Its core is grounded philosophically in swing (yure) theory, structurally in the circle-of-5ths "D progression hierarchy," and practically in highly engineered writing rules. Compared to the Soviet Sponsobin system and traditional Western tonal harmony systems, it possesses significant advancements in theoretical self-consistency, writing reproducibility, and formalized expression, but also carries limitations such as narrow applicability, terminological isolation, and model singularity. The details are as follows:

## I. Advancements Compared to Traditional Systems

### 1. Highly Self-Consistent Theoretical Ontology: "Swing" Unifies All Harmonic Phenomena

Traditional Western systems and the Sponsobin system both split "chord proper" and "non-chord tones" into two independent modules — chords are the structural body, non-chord tones are additional ornamentation, and the two are logically severed.
The Shimaoka system, by contrast, uses the pendulum-style swing logic of "rest position → displacement → resolution" to unify all phenomena from single-tone ornamentation to overall harmonic motion:
Stable chords are "rest positions"; functional connections between chords are broad swing undulations;
Passing tones, neighbor figures, appoggiaturas, suspensions, and other non-chord tones are all single-tone-level "displacements" (deviations from rest), inevitably "resolving" back to rest-position tones;
"Incidental chords" temporarily formed during ornamentation must still be understood by reduction to the originating chord, possessing no independent functional identity.
This design thoroughly avoids the conceptual debate of "do non-chord tones count as part of the chord or not," bringing all harmonic phenomena into a unified dynamic framework of "stability – deviation – return." Theclosed-loop character of its ontological logic far exceeds that of traditional tripartite functional systems.

### 2. Quantified Functional Hierarchy: Replacing Fuzzy Functional Grouping

Sponsobin's T/S/D functional groups and Western Riemannian functional theory both employ ternary functional grouping, perpetually plagued by extensive functional overlap and boundary ambiguity (e.g., in Sponsobin, VI simultaneously belongs to both the T group and the S group; the functional attribution of III is a longstanding controversy).
The Shimaoka system, based on the distance measured in circle-of-5ths D progressions (ascending 5ths), places all degrees on a linear tension hierarchy of D1–D6:
D1 (Ⅴ degree): 1 D-progression step from the tonic, highest tension, strongest resolution tendency
D2 (Ⅱ degree): 2 D-progression steps from the tonic
……
D6 (Ⅳ degree): 6 D-progression steps from the tonic; subdominant function is absorbed into the farthest D tier
The return path of all unstable chords is a unidirectional "stepwise resolution toward the tonic." Each degree's level of instability and resolution direction has a clear quantified position; there are no functional-overlap gray zones. The gradient explanatory power for harmonic tension far exceeds the traditional tripartite approach.

### 3. Systematic "Form" Concept: Precise Evolution of Chord Structure

The Shimaoka system calls the structural form of a chord its "form" and establishes strict form-evolution rules, rather than the scattered empirical descriptions of "omitted tones / added tones" found in traditional systems:
Core forms cover three categories — triad, 7th chord, 9th chord. "Ascension" (3→7→9) is unconditionally permitted; "descension" has explicit prohibitions (9→3 forbidden, 7→3 forbidden, 9→7 permitted);
The rootless dominant (root omission) is systematized as an independent formal category. Both the dominant 7th and dominant 9th have corresponding rootless forms, with complete supporting rules for disposition, inversion, and resolution — not simply "a chord with the root omitted";
5th omission and added tones (+4, +6) all have explicit formal boundaries, strictly distinguished from the core forms.
By comparison, the Sponsobin system's omitted tones are mostly empirical supplements, and the 9th chord is only treated as extended content; the Western system, though it has extended chords, treats them more as Romantic stylistic extensions and has not formed such a rigorous formal-evolution logic within the Classical four-part framework.

### 4. Engineered Writing Workflow: Procedural, Verifiable, Reproducible

This is the most outstanding advantage of the Shimaoka system — it decomposes four-part writing into a standardized workflow approaching "programming specifications," with extremely strong reproducibility — a quality possessed by neither Sponsobin nor Western textbooks:
Skeleton-layer standardization: proposes the "cadential unit" concept; all harmonic passages can be reducible to finite basic models such as K1 (T-D-T), K2 (T-D₂-D-T), K3 (T-S-T); complex passages are merely cadential chains of these units.
Cadence formula modularization: the core D→T connection is decomposed into 7 bass formulas × 8 soprano formulas; the cadence formula (D₂→D→T) is decomposed into 5 soprano × 2 bass fixed combinations — modular selection is possible directly during writing.
Rule system verifiability: all writing rules arereducible to seven major categories — A (Disposition), B (Motion), C (Simultaneous Motion), D (Bass), E (Disposition Changes), F (Ornament Disposition), G (Ornament Simultaneous Motion) — each with explicit judgment criteria and appendices, eliminating dependence on subjective teacher judgment for right and wrong.
Ornament boundaries are clear: all displacements (ornamental tones) are classified by form into six types, each with corresponding disposition, motion, and simultaneous-motion rules; even secondary displacement has explicit notation — far more systematic than the ornament classification in Sponsobin and Western textbooks.
This highly formalized nature also makes it naturally suited for programmatic generation and LLM structured processing — the plain-text notation system you are designing is essentially an extension of this system's formalizability.

### 5. Unified Explanation of Special Harmonies

For augmented 6th chords, Neapolitan chords, parallel major-minor modal interchange, and other special harmonies, the Shimaoka system can incorporate them all into a unified explanatory framework, reducing the number of system special cases:
Augmented 6th chords are categorized as D₂-functional variants of "dominant 7th chord with ♭5" — no separate chapter needed;
The Neapolitan chord is a forced-major-triad form on the lowered II degree, belonging to a D₂-tier altered variant;
Parallel major-minor interchange is systematized as "quasi chords," with consistent bidirectional borrowing logic rather than scattered "borrowed chords."

## II. Inherent Shortcomings of the System

### 1. Extremely Narrow Applicable Stylistic Range

The Shimaoka system is rigorously constructed around the four-part writing of the high Viennese Classical period. Beyond this range, applicability drops precipitously:
Chord forms are defined only up to the 9th chord; there are no 11th or 13th chords or other Romantic extended chords. Added tones are limited to the two basic forms of +4 and +6, and cannot cover Romantic chromatic harmony or Impressionist harmony;
Modulation covers only closely related modulation and pivot-key modulation; treatment of distant modulation and enharmonic modulation is very thin, far less rich than the Western system;
Explanatory power for Baroque polyphonic style and Romantic free harmony is insufficient; all non-circle-of-5ths progressions are difficult to incorporate into the D-tier model.
By comparison, the Sponsobin system can cover most tonal music from Baroque to Romantic; the Western system extends even further into 20th-century tonal music — much stronger universality.

### 2. Singular Functional Model with Explanatory Blind Spots

The linear hierarchical model, using the circle-of-5ths D progression as its sole core, has obvious theoretical blind spots:
Subdominant function is weakened: IV (S) is classified as D6, becoming the unstable tier farthest from the tonic. Plagal cadences, Phrygian cadences, and other subdominant-led progressions are merely supplementary and cannot attain theoretical status equal to the D function — explanatory power for subdominant-dominant styles is very weak.
Third-related harmonies are difficult to incorporate: all functions are derived from the circle of 5ths; third-related chord progressions and mediant-key harmonic alternation cannot be reasonably explained by the D-tier model and can only be treated as exceptions.
Altered-chord attribution is forced: aside from augmented 6th chords, other altered chords feel strained when forcibly incorporated into the 5th-tier hierarchy; they are handled more clearly by Western systems treating altered chords as an independent category.

### 3. Closed Terminological System, Poor Cross-System Compatibility

The Shimaoka system has independently created an entire naming system detached from internationally standard harmonic terminology:
The universal "chord inversion" is called something else entirely;
The universal "non-chord tones" are called "displacements" and further subdivided into appoggiaturas, escape tones, anticipations, etc., under Japanese-style naming;
"Pedal point" is called "sustained bass"; "borrowed chord" is called "quasi chord"; "root-omitted chord" is called "rootless dominant chord."
This terminological set is internally logically consistent but completely incompatible with global mainstream harmony teaching systems; learning costs are high, and cross-academic-system exchange is difficult. Although the Sponsobin system has Soviet characteristics, its core terminology (T, S, D, triad, inversion) is basically aligned with the Western system — much better compatibility.

### 4. Overly Detailed Rules Leading to Insufficient Flexibility and Deviation from Actual Works

High engineering is a double-edged sword: the finer the rules, the lower the tolerance for actual works.
For example, the strict prohibition of formal descension "7→3" and "9→3" ignores the many exceptions in actual Classical works where such voice-leading simplification occurs;
Judgments on consecutive 5ths and voice-leading are too dogmatic; the stylistic handling of many composers would be judged as "violations";
Beginners must memorize vast numbers of rules and appendices; the entry barrier is high, and it is easy to fall into "rule-worship," neglecting musical style and expressivity.
The Western system emphasizes principle and stylistic flexibility; the Sponsobin system also retains more empirical exceptions — both better fit the diversity of actual works.

## Summary

The Shimaoka system is essentially a **"highly formalized teaching and production system specialized in Classical four-part writing"**: it achieves extreme rigor, self-consistency, and reproducibility within a limited scope of harmonic problems, making it exceptionally well-suited for standardized teaching, automated writing, and LLM structured processing; but the trade-off is the sacrifice of universality and stylistic flexibility. Beyond the scope of Classical four-part writing, its explanatory power and applicability decline rapidly.
