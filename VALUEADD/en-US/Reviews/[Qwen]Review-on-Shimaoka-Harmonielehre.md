The Shimaoka harmony system, as the pedagogical core of the Tokyo University of the Arts, indeed offers a perspective fundamentally distinct from the Sponsobin system or traditional Western functional harmony theory. Its advancements lie primarily in its unique theoretical model and notational system, though these may also present certain learning challenges.

### ✨ Advancements: A Distinctive Theoretical Perspective and Notation

The most central advancement of the Shimaoka system lies in its **"Swing (yure) Theory."**

1.  **The Dynamic "Swing" Model**
    *   **Core idea**: This theory likens harmonic motion to a pendulum, swinging back and forth between stable "rest positions" (prototype chords) and unstable "displacements." Harmonic progression is no longer a simple connection of functions but a dynamic process from tension to resolution, from deviation to return.
    *   **Advantage**: This model provides an exceptionally intuitive and unified framework for understanding displacement and ornamental tones (such as passing tones, auxiliary tones, appoggiaturas). Elements treated as "non-chord tones" in traditional theory become, in the Shimaoka system, natural components of "swing" — the very forces that generate tension and propel the music forward.

2.  **Functionalized Degree Symbols**
    *   **Core idea**: The system uses `Ⅰ, Ⅱ, Ⅲ, Ⅳ, Ⅴ, Ⅵ, Ⅶ` to mark scale degrees and assigns them explicit functional attributes. For example, `Ⅴ` is the Dominant function (D), `Ⅳ` is the Subdominant function (S), and `Ⅱ` is defined as the Second Dominant function (D₂).
    *   **Advantage**: This notation tightly integrates a chord's "identity" (scale degree) with its "role" (function). In particular, explicitly defining the `Ⅱ` chord as the D₂ function emphasizes its strong tendency to lead to the Dominant (`Ⅴ`), making the logic of the classic cadential progression `Ⅰ` → `Ⅱ` → `Ⅴ` → `Ⅰ` (K2 cadential unit) exceptionally clear.

3.  **Systematized "Cadential Units" and "Cadence Formulae"**
    *   **Core idea**: The theory formalizes common harmonic progressions as "cadential units" (e.g., K1: `Ⅰ`→`Ⅴ`→`Ⅰ`, K2: `Ⅰ`→`Ⅱ`→`Ⅴ`→`Ⅰ`) and refines cadences into multiple "formulae" (e.g., Dominant formula, Subdominant formula).
    *   **Advantage**: This provides a highly structured and actionable set of "templates" for harmonic writing and analysis. Learners can construct phrases by combining different cadential units and cadence formulae like building blocks, significantly lowering the entry barrier to four-part writing while ensuring logical coherence.

4.  **A Plain-Text Notation System Optimized for Modern Technology**
    *   **Core idea**: The plain-text notation designed by Shiki Suen (e.g., `rⅤ7`, `+Ⅳ`, `pⅡ1`) is a modernized transcription of the Shimaoka system's notation.
    *   **Advantage**: It dramatically enhances the theory's usability in digital environments. This notation is precise, unambiguous, and highly suited for text-based communication, programmatic processing, and AI learning — solving the pain point of traditional harmony symbols being difficult to type and search in plain text.

### 🤔 Possible Shortcomings or Challenges

Despite its many strengths, the Shimaoka system may also present some challenges:

1.  **High Entry Barrier and Terminological Wall**
    *   For learners already familiar with Roman numeral analysis or the Sponsobin system, a complete switch to an entirely new terminological system (such as "swing," "rest position," "D₂ function," etc.) is required. As you noted, terminological isolation between different theoretical systems is critically important; forced mixing may lead to conceptual confusion.

2.  **Notational Complexity**
    *   While the plain-text notation is machine-friendly, it is extremely information-dense for human reading. For instance, a single symbol like `mrⅤ93@Ⅴ/Ⅴ` encapsulates borrowing, omission, chord type, inversion, tonicization target, and sustained bass — a beginner may need considerable time to parse it.

3.  **Geographic Specificity of the Theory**
    *   The Shimaoka system has deep influence primarily within Japanese music education (especially the Tokyo University of the Arts). In international academic exchange or contexts using more universal Western harmony theory, its concepts and notation may not be widely understood, which to some extent limits its universality.

In summary, the advancement of the Shimaoka harmony system lies in its provision of an exceptionally logical and dynamic harmonic analysis model, solidified through a rigorous notational system. It is more akin to an **"operating manual" designed for composition and writing**, whereas the Sponsobin system is more akin to an **"interpretive framework" designed for analyzing Classical works**. Each has its emphasis; which one to choose depends on the learner's goals.
