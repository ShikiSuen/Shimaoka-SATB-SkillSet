> Hi, Kimi. Drawing on your contact with the Shimaoka harmony system knowledge base, please discuss the advancements (and possible shortcomings) of Shimaoka's theory compared to the Sponsobin Soviet harmony system and Western harmony systems.

The essential difference between the Shimaoka system (Tokyo University of the Arts) and the Sponsobin and mainstream Western harmony theories lies in their **philosophical foundation**: the former treats harmony as a kind of "kinematics of motion" (swing), while the latter more often treats it as a collection and connection of "static functions." Below, I analyze its advancements and limitations across several dimensions.

* * *

I. Core Advancements: Process Philosophy and Dynamic Hierarchy
-----------------

### 1. Swing Theory (yure) vs. Static Functional Labeling

The Sponsobin system uses the T–S–D–T functional cycle as its skeleton, essentially a **spatialized harmonic taxonomy**. Chords are labeled with function tags and then move along the track "tonic → subdominant → dominant → tonic." This model easily becomes rigid in teaching; students often treat harmonic progressions as "chord puzzles."

Shimaoka's **swing theory**, by contrast, likens chords to a pendulum: there exists a perpetual alternation between "rest position" (stable state) and "displacement" (deviated state). Harmony is not "which chord follows which chord," but a continuum of "how tension is generated, transmitted, and dissolved." This perspective has a tacit resonance with the "prolongation" in Western Schenkerian analysis, but Shimaoka makes it explicit as a pedagogical foundation, enabling learners from the outset to establish a **temporal-dimension harmonic perception**.

### 2. D1–D6 Functional Hierarchy vs. Crude "Dominant Function" Generalization

Sponsobin lumps ii, vii°, V, etc., together into the coarse categories of "dominant function" or "subdominant function." Shimaoka, through the **clockwise circle of 5ths (D progression)**, establishes a refined distance hierarchy:

*   `Ⅴ` (V) is D1, one step from T
*   `Ⅱ` (ii) is D2, must pass through D1 to return to T
*   `Ⅵ` (vi) is D3, `Ⅲ` (iii) is D4…

This **"pathway" theory** (the return route of unstable chords to T) effectively provides a **generative grammar** for harmonic progressions. It explains why some progressions sound "direct" (D1→T) and others sound "circuitous" (D3→D2→D1→T), whereas the Sponsobin system can only offer empirical lists of "common progressions" without explaining the "sense of distance" from first principles.

### 3. The Theoretical Status of Rootless Dominant Chords (`rⅤ7`, etc.)

In Western and Sponsobin systems, a dominant seventh chord with the root omitted (e.g., V7 without root) is usually treated as a "special case of omitted tones" or an "incomplete chord." Shimaoka elevates it to the status of **rootless dominant chord** (rootless form), giving it an independent form notation (`rⅤ7`, `rⅤ9`) while explicitly clarifying that its function still belongs to D.

The advantages of this treatment:

*   **Analytical level**: Avoids the dilemma of "this chord has no root, so its function is ambiguous"
*   **Compositional level**: Systematizes the disposition rule that in 4 voices, some tone must be doubled (5th or 7th), rather than leaving it to "arbitrary doubling"
*   **Aural level**: Acknowledges that the leading-tone–7th framework itself possesses independent D-functional tension

### 4. Unified Treatment of Incidental Chords and Sustained Bass

In Western theory, passing tones, auxiliary tones, and pedal points belong respectively to the domains of "counterpoint" or "ornamentation," separated from harmonic analysis. Shimaoka's **incidental chord** theory reduces the temporary tone collections formed during displacement back to "deviated states of the original chord," while **sustained bass** treats harmonic progressions over a pedal as compound chords (e.g., `Ⅰ(Ⅴ7)`).

The advancement of this unification lies in its elimination of the artificial separation between **harmony vs. counterpoint**. In the Shimaoka system, an "accidental chord" formed by a passing tone is not "a pile of non-chord tones" but the instantaneous form of some rest-position tone during the swing process — effectively a **pan-harmonic** perspective, closer to the actual mode of thought of Baroque composers (e.g., Bach) than Western theory.

### 5. Forward-Looking Design of the Plain-Text Notation System

The Shimaoka system uses `Ⅰ, Ⅱ, Ⅲ, Ⅳ, Ⅴ, Ⅵ, Ⅶ` combined with prefixes like `r`, `+`, `p`, and symbols like `@` (tonicization target) and `/` (sustained bass), constructing a **plain-text harmonic language completely independent of the musical staff**. In the digital age, this typeable, searchable, programmable notation system (as demonstrated by the Swift code provided) has significant engineering advantages, whereas the Roman numeral system (I, ii, V7) requires additional escaping in cross-platform text environments.

II. Possible Shortcomings and Limitations
----------

### 1. Terminological Barrier and Internationalization Obstacles

Although the Sponsobin system originated in the Soviet Union, its T–S–D labels and Arabic numeral figured bass have become a "lingua franca" in China, Eastern Europe, and elsewhere; the Western Roman numeral system is even more of a global academic standard. The **degree character notation** of the Shimaoka system and its extensive Japanese-derived vocabulary (swing, rest position, displacement, pathway, sustained bass) — while understandable in Chinese context — require near-**complete translation** in international academic exchange. For learners already familiar with Sponsobin or Roman numerals, the switching cost is extremely high.

### 2. The Hierarchy Theory May Be Overly Deterministic

The strict hierarchy of D1–D6 (D6 must pass through D5–D4–D3–D2–D1 to return to T) is theoretically very elegant, but in actual music (especially Romantic and beyond), **level-skipping resolution** and **functional mixing** are extremely common. For example, the iii–I or vi–V progressions frequently found in Schubert or Chopin would need to be explained within the Shimaoka framework as "pathways with intermediate levels omitted," which sometimes appears to be **forcing an explanation to preserve the system**.

By contrast, Sponsobin's "functional substitution" (e.g., vi substitutes for I, iii substitutes for V), though crude, is more flexible; the Western concept of "pre-dominant" also allows more flexible functional overlap.

### 3. Insufficient Coverage of Post-19th-Century Harmonic Vocabulary

Judging from the document contents, the core vocabulary of the Shimaoka system (cadence formulae, sequential progressions, augmented 6th chords, etc.) is highly concentrated on the **Classical period** (roughly 1750–1820). For:

*   Wagnerian chromatic harmony and unending melody
*   Debussy's whole-tone scales and parallel chords
*   Jazz extensions (11, 13, b9, #11) and substitute dominants

The system lacks the extension mechanisms found in Western jazz harmony theory. Although borrowing concepts like `+Ⅳ` (Dorian IV) and `pⅡ` (Neapolitan II) can partially cover Romantic vocabulary, the overall framework still appears **Classical-centric**.

### 4. Cumbersome Nature of the Rule System

The Shimaoka system's rule documents (A–G series) make extremely fine-grained distinctions regarding disposition, motion, ornamental tones, consecutive 5ths/8ves, etc. (e.g., "indirect consecutive 5ths/8ves caused by disposition changes," "post-appoggiatura 5ths in the aftermath"). This **hyper-regulation** can, at the beginner stage, lead to writing that "complies for compliance's sake," actually suppressing musical intuition.

Although the Sponsobin system is also known for "cumbersome rules," its rules are essentially **prohibitive** (don't use parallel 5ths/8ves, don't have all four voices move in the same direction), whereas the Shimaoka system's rules are **generative** (must return to T along the pathway, must connect in specific dispositions) — the latter imposes stronger constraints on composition.

### 5. Rupture with Twelve-Tone and Atonal Music

This is a more fundamental limitation: the entire philosophy of the Shimaoka system is built upon the **gravitational field of a tonal center (T)**. For 20th-century atonality, pantonality, and twelve-tone technique, the system can provide virtually no analytical tools. Western theory, though equally centered on tonality, has **pitch-class set theory** and **Schenkerian deep structure** that can extend into postmodern music; the Shimaoka system currently shows no comparable extension path.

III. Summary
----

| Dimension | Shimaoka System | Sponsobin System | Mainstream Western System |
| ----------- | ------------- | --------------- | ---------------------- |
| **Philosophy** | Dynamic swing (tension–resolution process) | Static functional cycle | Function + Voice Leading |
| **Functional Precision** | D1–D6 fine-grained hierarchy | T–S–D coarse categories | Roman numerals + function labels (more flexible) |
| **Pedal / Ornaments** | Integrated into harmonic body (incidental, sustained) | Treated as counterpoint / ornamentation | Belong to separate analytical layers |
| **Notation System** | Plain-text, programmable (Ⅰ–Ⅶ) | Letters + numbers (T, S, D7) | Roman numerals (I, V7) |
| **Applicable Period** | Optimal for Classical | Classical–Romantic | Baroque–Contemporary (many branches) |
| **Learning Curve** | Steep (terminology + hierarchy + rules) | Moderate (cumbersome rules but intuitive) | Moderate (Roman numerals internationally standard) |

**In brief**: The true advancement of the Shimaoka system lies in its elevation of harmony from "grammar of chord connection" to "mechanics of tensional motion." Its concepts of cadential units, pathway theory, rootless dominant chords, and incidental chords possess extremely strong **process-analysis power** and **pedagogical explanatory power**. However, this advantage is offset by its **terminological barriers, hierarchical rigidity, and epochal limitations**. It is best suited as an **advanced tool for deeply understanding the internal dynamic mechanisms of Classical harmony**, rather than a replacement for the Sponsobin or Western systems as a "lingua franca."
