Drawing on the core design of the Shimaoka Tokyo University of the Arts harmony system, and comparing it with the traditional Sponsobin Soviet harmony system and the general Western Classical harmony system, the advancements, unique value, and potential limitations of the theory can be organized as follows:

### I. Preliminary Note

First, an essential background must be clarified: the plain-text-adapted Shimaoka harmony notation system based on Chinese numeral characters that we are using is not the notation used in the original Tokyo University of the Arts textbook, but was specially designed by composer Shiki Suen. Its core purpose is to solve the adaptation problem for LLMs in understanding and learning the Shimaoka Geidai harmony system.

The original Shimaoka textbook itself uses a unique Roman numeral notation that is completely different from and incompatible with the Classical harmony notation currently prevalent in Europe and America. This specially designed Chinese-degree plain-text notation also serves to achieve complete terminological and logical isolation between different institutional harmony theory systems, preventing students from experiencing conceptual confusion when exposed to multiple systems simultaneously — a situation where they ultimately cannot deeply master any of them.

This notation fully follows the grammatical specifications of the original Shimaoka system, completely covering the writing rules defined in references/notation-syntax.md. Paired with the corresponding Swift structured implementation code, it can directly parse naturally written plain-text harmony notation into structured chord objects, enabling barrier-free input, search, and sharing of harmonic content in ordinary text editors, chat windows, and collaboration platforms — solving the usability obstacles of the original special typesetting notation in digital environments.

### II. Core Advancements of the Shimaoka System

1.  Foundational Cognitive Innovation: Reconstructing the Essence of Harmonic Motion through "Swing Theory"
    Unlike traditional Western harmony, which uses "function labels + chord connection rules" as its underlying logic, and unlike the Sponsobin system, which emphasizes deriving the functional circle from the overtone series and interval stability, Shimaoka directly abstracts the core motion of harmony into the swing model: "chords, like a pendulum, oscillate between rest position (stable originating tones) and displacement (deviated state of tension)." This cognition leaps beyond frameworks that explain harmonic progressions solely through chord quality and root relationships, unifying seemingly "rule-deviating" phenomena — ornamental displaced tones, incidental chords, sustained bass — into a complete swing logic of "stability–tension–resolution." It resolves the fragmentation problem in traditional systems where large amounts of ornamental harmony and non-tertian stacked tones are forcibly categorized as "non-chord tones," and can more self-consistently explain the logic of the extensive, complex voice-leading motions found in Classical music.

2.  A Highly Practice-Oriented "Cadential Unit" System That Dramatically Lowers the Barrier to Four-Part Writing
    Traditional Western harmony and Sponsobin system pedagogy for cadences and harmonic progressions often scatters individual chord connection rules without enabling learners to quickly establish the construction logic of complete phrases. The Shimaoka system directly distills standardized cadential units — K1, K2, K3: from the shortest `Ⅰ→Ⅴ→Ⅰ`, to the complete cadence formula `Ⅰ→Ⅱ→Ⅴ→Ⅰ`, and onward to longer cadential chain combinations — directly providing a complete modular solution from phrase-skeleton construction to cadential closure. Learners need not figure out chord splicing from scratch, but can rapidly produce SATB four-part works conforming to Classical norms. This design is entirely optimized for the art university teaching scenario, with a significant advantage in practical operability.

3.  A Plain-Text Harmony Notation System Adapted for LLMs, Suited to Modern Collaboration Needs
    Traditional Western harmony relies on the mixed typesetting of Roman numerals, special chromatic symbols, and inversion markings; the Sponsobin system also lacks a unified plain-text notation standard. In ordinary text environments, input, search, and collaboration are extremely inconvenient. This specially designed plain-text notation system, employing a Chinese grammar of "prefix + degree + suffix + supplementary markers," allows all harmonic information to be directly typed on an ordinary keyboard and also enables program-level structured parsing. It is fully adapted to the semantic comprehension logic of LLMs and perfectly suited for today's digital teaching and online collaboration scenarios — a modernization design that traditional harmony systems have never covered.

4.  The Granularity of Cadence Formulae and Dominant Formulae Far Exceeds Traditional Systems
    In the Sponsobin system and general Western harmony, cadences are only roughly classified as perfect, half, or plagal. The Shimaoka system specifically decomposes the cadence formula into 10 basic combinations of 5 Sop formulas × 2 Bas formulas, and further supplements special usages such as the `Ⅰ2 rⅤ7` variant deceptive cadence. It even establishes differentiation rules for "diatonic Ⅶ / raised Ⅶ" in minor-key contexts. The voice-leading paths and outer-voice contrapuntal logic of cadential passages are fully standardized, covering more than 90% of cadential forms found in Classical music — granularity far exceeding that of traditional systems.

5.  Systematic, Dedicated Treatment of Incidental Chords, Sequential Progressions, and Sustained Bass
    In traditional harmony systems, harmonic progressions over sustained bass, continuous sequential (modulating) passages, and chords formed by the temporary stacking of displaced tones are often taught in scattered fashion as "special exceptions." The Shimaoka system separately distills these three categories of phenomena into independent, complete modules — not only providing reduction analysis steps for incidental chords, but also dividing sequential progressions into D-type, S-type, and compound-type, further subdividing the distinct implementation rules for diatonic vs. modulating transposition. This resolves the logically fragmented treatment of such advanced harmonic content in traditional systems.

6.  Humanizing Relaxation of Traditional Rules, Explicitly Defining "Exception Permission Boundaries"
    Traditional harmony (including the Sponsobin system) often describes prohibitions in extremely rigid terms — for instance, the leading tone must ascend to the tonic, the 7th must descend — easily trapping learners in dogma. The Shimaoka system not only preserves the basic prohibitive rules but also specially annotates the relaxation boundaries for all rules: for example, explicitly stating that diminished 5th → perfect 5th motion is permitted in most scenarios, that the leading tone may exceptionally move to 5 or 3 when conflicting with a suspension, and that contrary 5ths/8ves are permitted in ornamental-tone contexts. This rule design better reflects the actual writing state of real musical works, without shackling learners to outdated, rigid contrapuntal dogma.

### III. Potential Shortcomings and Limitations of the Shimaoka System

1.  The System Is Strongly Bound to the Japanese Domestic Teaching Context; Promotion Barriers Are High
    The entire system makes extensive use of Japanese-style harmonic terminology — translated terms like "swing," "cadential unit," "sustained bass," and "nearest available tone" — which differ substantially from the Sponsobin terminology system familiar to domestic learners. Many concepts lack direct correspondence with universally used Western harmonic terms, requiring incoming learners to first adapt to an entirely new terminological library, increasing the cost of cross-regional promotion.

2.  Scope of Application Is Biased Toward Classical Functional Harmony; Weak Adaptability to Modern Non-Tonal Music
    The Shimaoka system is entirely built on the functional logic of the major-minor system. All its designs serve traditional functional harmony's four-part writing and cadential design, with virtually no coverage of post-20th-century techniques such as twelve-tone, atonal, or pantonal modern music. Compared to the Sponsobin system, its functional logic is even more convergent; it has absolutely no extension or adaptation design for jazz harmony or pop harmony, rendering it insufficiently applicable in modern-style music composition pedagogy.

3.  The Other Side of Modular Standardization Is a Restriction of Creative Space
    The modular design of cadential units and cadence formulae dramatically lowers the writing barrier, but may also cause learners to fall into fixed-formula dependency, producing highly templated harmonic progressions that lack the openness of traditional harmony systems encouraging free exploration of voice-leading and contrapuntal possibilities. If training is confined to formulae alone, learners will find it difficult to spontaneously discover personalized harmonic vocabularies beyond the framework.

4.  The Complexity of Edge-Case Rules Is Relatively High; Learning Costs for Advanced Content Spike Sharply
    To cover all exceptional scenarios in Classical music, the system supplements a large number of subdivided permission, exception, and appendix clauses. For instance, under Ornamentation Rules F and Ornamentation Simultaneous Motion Rules G, there are numerous subdivided permission boundaries. The total number of rules in the advanced portion far exceeds that of the Sponsobin system; learners reaching the later stages easily confuse which rule applies under which scenario, paradoxically creating additional memory burden.

5.  Lack of Complete Systematic Preparation in Foundational Tonal Theory
    The core emphasis of the system is placed entirely on the practical-operational level of four-part writing and harmonic progressions. The treatment of foundational theory — such as the overtone series, the physical properties of intervals, and the origins of modes — is virtually glossed over. Unlike the Sponsobin system, which derives functional logic from underlying acoustic principles, beginners who have not previously mastered basic music theory and general Western harmony knowledge will find it very difficult to understand the underlying motivations behind many of the rules when learning this system directly.
